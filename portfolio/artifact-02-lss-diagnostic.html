Five Operations Bottlenecks Every Indian MSME Should Fix
Before Adding AI
— A Lean Six Sigma Diagnostic

Chandra Shekhar IIMB EGMP Anthropic AI Fluency Lean Six Sigma Expert May 2026

Most Indian SME founders start the AI conversation by asking: “Which tool should I buy?” That is the wrong question. The right question is: “Are my operations ready for AI to work on?” In Lean Six Sigma terms, AI is a process optimisation tool. And process optimisation on a broken process does not fix the process — it accelerates the breakage. Here are the five bottlenecks to clear first.

B1 Unmeasured variation in your core process

In LSS terms, this is a lack of baseline data. You cannot improve what you have not measured, and AI cannot optimise a process where the inputs and outputs are not tracked consistently.

The symptom in most ₹5–50Cr businesses: the team has a rough sense of how long things take, but no one has measured cycle time, defect rate, or throughput for any process in writing. When asked “how long does order fulfillment take?” the answer is “usually 3 days, sometimes 7, depends on the vendor.”

An AI forecasting or automation tool pointed at this process will inherit the variation. It will confidently produce outputs that swing between accurate and wrong — and your team will not know which is which.

Fix first: Pick your highest-volume process. Measure its cycle time and error rate for 30 days. Write it down. That baseline is the starting point for every improvement — AI or otherwise.

B2 Process steps living in people’s heads, not on paper

This is the documentation gap — and it is the single most consistent finding when I audit operations in Indian SMEs. Processes exist. They work. They are just not written anywhere that a new joiner, a machine, or an AI model can read them.

The consequence is more severe than it looks. When your senior ops person is on leave, work slows down or stops. When you try to scale from one city to two, the second location runs differently because the process was never documented — only demonstrated. When you plug in an AI assistant, it has nothing to read. It gives generic answers because it has no access to your specific processes, your vendors, your exception rules.

AI tools — whether Claude, ChatGPT, or any Indian alternative — work by reading your documentation. No documentation means no grounding. A system without grounding hallucinates. That is not an AI failure; it is a documentation failure.

Fix first: Run a process inventory. List every core process in your business. Mark each one: “Documented” or “In someone’s head.” If more than 40% are undocumented, write those SOPs before any AI pilot. This is the single highest-leverage preparation step.

B3 The founder in the critical path of routine decisions

This is one of the most common — and least discussed — bottlenecks in Indian family and founder-led businesses. The founder approves every PO above a threshold. Every vendor payment. Every customer discount. Every hire shortlist. Every contract renewal.

In LSS terms, this is a queue bottleneck at a single resource. The process stalls every time that resource is unavailable. The team has learned to wait. Work-in-progress piles up on the founder’s desk — or WhatsApp.

The AI trap here is seductive: “We’ll build an AI dashboard that gives me all the information faster, so I can decide faster.” That is a speed improvement on a bottleneck that should be eliminated, not accelerated. Faster approvals by the founder is not delegation — it is a more efficient centralisation.

AI cannot sign off on a vendor payment. You can, and you must build the documented decision rules that allow someone else to.

Fix first: Map which decisions require the founder versus which decisions have a rule that allows delegation. Write the rule for three of the highest-volume founder-decision categories. Implement for 60 days. Only then look at AI-assisted approval workflows — because now there is a rule for the AI to apply.

B4 No feedback loop: work goes out, results never come back

In LSS DMAIC methodology, this is the “Control” phase gap. Many Indian SMEs are good at doing the work. They are inconsistent at measuring whether the work achieved the outcome, and almost universally poor at routing that outcome data back to the person or process that generated the output.

Examples: A sales team sends 50 proposals. Nobody tracks which ones converted, which objections lost the deal, or what the average conversion rate is by product type. A manufacturing unit ships 200 units. Customer returns come in, but the return reasons are not coded or trended — they go straight to the replacement dispatch queue. A service team resolves customer complaints. Resolution time is tracked; re-escalation rate is not.

An AI model trained on this data will optimise for what it can see. If conversion data does not come back, the AI cannot improve the proposal. If return reasons are not coded, the AI cannot flag the pattern. Garbage-in, garbage-out is the oldest rule in computing. It applies equally to AI.

Fix first: For each of your top three processes, define one feedback metric. Then create a simple mechanism — a weekly log, a return-reason code, a follow-up call result field — that captures it. Run it manually for 8 weeks. After that, AI can automate the capture and analysis. Before that, the AI has nothing reliable to work with.

B5 No named owner, no defined success metric for the AI project itself

This is the project governance bottleneck — and it kills more AI initiatives than any technical failure.

The pattern: a founder gets excited about AI at a seminar or from a LinkedIn post. They tell the team “let’s explore this.” A vendor demo happens. A trial starts. Three months later, the tool is installed, occasionally used, and nobody is quite sure whether it is working. The founder moves on to the next priority. The tool subscription auto-renews.

In LSS terms, this is a project without a Define phase. No problem statement. No metric. No owner. No timeline.

Every successful operational change — AI or otherwise — needs one sentence that the team can point to: “By [date], [metric] will improve from [current baseline] to [target], and [name] is accountable.”

If you cannot write that sentence for your AI initiative, you are not ready to launch it. You are ready to spend money on it — which is different.

Fix first: Write the sentence. One metric. One name. One date. If you cannot agree on those three in your leadership team within one meeting, that disagreement is the real problem — and resolving it is more valuable than any AI tool you could buy this month.

Pre-AI Diagnostic Checklist

Run this before committing budget to any AI tool or pilot. Score yourself honestly. One tick per item.

B1 — Measurement

We have measured cycle time for our highest-volume process in the last 90 days.
We have a documented defect or error rate for at least one core process.
We know our process throughput (units/orders/tickets per week) without having to call someone.

B2 — Documentation

60%+ of our core operational processes are written down in current, accurate SOPs.
A new joiner can complete our top-3 processes using our documentation without asking a senior.
We have documented our vendor exception rules, customer escalation rules, and pricing authority limits.

B3 — Delegation

Fewer than 3 routine decision categories require the founder’s sign-off as the only authorised approver.
We have written delegation rules for at least 3 high-frequency decision types.
When the founder is unavailable for 2 days, work continues at 80%+ normal throughput.

B4 — Feedback loops

We track at least one outcome metric (not just output) for each of our top 3 processes.
Customer complaint/return reasons are coded, not just counted.
Outcome data reaches the team or person who generated the original work.

B5 — Project governance

We can write one sentence: “By [date], [metric] will improve from [X] to [Y], and [name] owns it.”
The AI project has one named owner who is not the founder.
We have defined what “this is not working” looks like and have a stop date if the metric is not moving.
Scoring:
13–15 ticks → Operationally ready. Proceed with AI pilot selection (see Artifact #1 for the 5-decision framework).
9–12 ticks → Partially ready. Fix the sections where you scored less than 2/3 before launching.
Below 9 → AI will compound your current problems, not solve them. Spend the next 60 days on operations fundamentals. The AI will still be there.

The honest summary

None of the five bottlenecks above are AI problems. They are operations problems that have existed for years, and AI has simply made them more visible because every vendor is now promising to solve them with a tool.

Lean Six Sigma was built on a simple observation: before you optimise a process, define it, measure it, and understand where variation comes from. That principle is 40 years old. It applies to AI implementation with identical force.

The Indian SMEs that get the most from AI in 2026 are the ones that already ran their operations with discipline — documented, measured, delegated, feedback-looped. They pick up AI tools and they work immediately, because the foundation was already solid.

If your foundation has gaps, this diagnostic tells you where they are. Fix those first. You will be a better business with or without AI — and a much better AI adopter when you are ready.
